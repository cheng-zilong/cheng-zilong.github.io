Chapter 4. Controllability and Observability

Posted on 2018-11-11 In EE5101 Linear Systems Views: Valine:

Controllability

  1. A LTI system is controllable if there exists an input u(t), \(0\le t\le t_1\) that drives the system from any initial state \(x(0)\) to any other state \(x(t_1)\) in a finite time \(t_1\) .Otherwise, the system is said to be uncontrollable.

  2. Controllable Condition

    1. controllability Grammian

      \[ W(0,t)=\int_{0}^{t}e^{A\tau}BB^Te^{A^{T}\tau}d\tau=\int_0^te^{A(t-\tau)}BB^Te^{A^T(t-\tau)}d\tau \] is nonsingular for all \(t\in[0,t_1]\). Then the control input is \[ u(t)=-B^Te^{A^T(t_1-t)}W^{-1}(0,t_1)(e^{At_1}x_0-x_1) \]

      Then the state can be proved, \[ x(t_1)=x_1 \]

    2. the \(n\times nr\) matrix \[ M_c=\begin{bmatrix} B&AB&A^2B &\dotsb&A^{n-1}B\end{bmatrix} \] is full row rank

Observability

  1. A linear time-invariant system is observable if every unknown initial state \(x(0)\) can be determined from the knowledge of \(u(t)\) and the observation of \(y(t)\) over finite time interval. Otherwise, the system is unobservable.

  2. Observability Condition

    1. Observability Grammian

      \[ \begin{array}{lcl} y(t) & = & Ce^{At}x(0)\\ e^{A^{T}t}C^T y(t) & = & e^{A^{T}t}C^T Ce^{At}x(0)\\ \displaystyle \int _0 ^t e^{A^{T}\tau}C^T y(\tau) d\tau & = & \displaystyle \int _0 ^t e^{A^{T}\tau}C^T Ce^{A\tau}x(0) d\tau \end{array} \]

      if we have $M(0,t) = _0 ^t e{A{T}}C^T Ce^{A} d$ \[ \begin{array}{} M(0,t)^{-1}\displaystyle \int _0 ^t e^{A^{T}\tau}C^T y(\tau) & = x(0) \end{array} \] therefore, if M(0,t) is nonsingular, the system is observable.

    2. the \(mn \times n\) observability matrix \[ M_o=\begin{bmatrix} C\\ CA\\ \vdots \\CA^{n-1} \end{bmatrix} \] is full column rank.

    3. the \((n+m) \times n\) matrix \[ \begin{bmatrix} A-\lambda I \\ C \end{bmatrix} \] has full column rank for every eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of A.

Kalman Decomposition

One system is denoted as S. If the system is not controllable, then \[ rank (M_c)=rank[B \quad AB \quad A^2B \quad \dotsm \quad A^{n-1}B]=n_1<n \] Then we can choose \[ P^{-1}=[p_1\quad\dotsm\quad p_{n_1}\quad\dotsm\quad p_n] \] where the last \((n-n_1)\) columns are arbitrary so long as \(P^{-1}\) is nonsingular.

Now we have a system \[ \dot x=Ax+Bu \\ y=Cx \\ \] It is uncontrollable. And we have transform P.

The system \(\bar S\) \[ \bar A=PAP^{-1} \\ \bar B=PB \\ \bar C=CP^{-1}\\ \bar D=D \\ \bar x=Px \\ x=P^{-1}\bar x \\ \dot {\bar x} =PAP^{-1}\bar x+PBu \\ y=CP^{-1}\bar x \] Controllability Matrix \[ M_c=[B \quad AB \quad A^2B \quad \dotsm \quad A^{n-1}B] \] However, then rank of \(W_c\) is \(n_i\) which is less than \(n\)

Now we choose the matrix \[ P^{-1}=[p_1 \quad p_2 \quad \dotsm \quad p_{n_i} \quad \dotsm \quad p_n] \] \(p_1\) ~ \(p_{n_i}\) are the independent vectors in \(W_c\), and other vectors are chosen arbitrarily so that \(P\) is nonsingular.

Now we have \(P^{-1}=[P_1 \quad P_2]\) , P1 are the independent vectors in \(W_c\). \(P_2\) are the vectors we choose arbitrarily.

We denote \(P=\begin{bmatrix} M \\ N\end{bmatrix}\) ,Then we have \[ PP^{-1}= \begin{bmatrix} MP_1 & MP_2 \\ NP_1 & NP_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I_1 & 0 \\ 0 & I_2 \end{bmatrix} \] We note that \(NP_1=0\) , We also find that B is in the range of \(P_1\) . \(p_1\) is B or part of B. \(NB=0\)

That means \[ \bar B=PB=\begin{bmatrix} M \\ N\end{bmatrix}B=\begin{bmatrix} MB \\ NB\end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} MB \\ 0\end{bmatrix} \] Now we need to prove \(AP_1 \in P_1\)

Using Caley-Hamilton Theorem, \[ P_1 \in \overbrace{\begin{bmatrix} B & AB & A^2B & \dotsm & A^nB\end{bmatrix}}^{W_C} \\ AP_1 \in \overbrace{\begin{bmatrix} AB & A^2B & A^3B & \dotsm & A^{n+1}B\end{bmatrix}}^{P+} \] Since \(P_1\) is the linear independent parts of \(W_c\) , the range of \(P_1\) is same as the range of \(W_c\) .

We only need to prove \(P^+ \in W_c\) then we can prove \(AP_1 \in P^+ \in P_1\)

Using Caley-Hamilton Theorem \[ A^{n+1}=a_0+a_1A+a_2A^2+\dotsm+a_nA^n \] Therefore \(AP_1 \in P_1\) \[ \bar A=PAP^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix} M \\ N\end{bmatrix}[AP_1 \quad AP_2] =\begin{bmatrix} MAP_1 & MAP_2 \\ NAP_1 & NAP_2\end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} MAP_1 & MAP_2 \\ 0 & NAP_2 \end{bmatrix} \] Kalman decomposition Q.E.D

Example \[ \dot x=Ax+Bu \\ y=Cx \\ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ B=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ C=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ M_c=\begin{bmatrix} B & AB & A^2B \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 1\\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \text{Uncontrollable}\\ P^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1\\ 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} P=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 & -1\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \bar A=PAP^{-1}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \bar B=PB=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\\ \bar C=CP^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \] Similarly, if a system is unobservable \[ rank (M_o)=n_2<n \] Choose \(n-n_2\) vectors in the null space of the observability matrix \(M_o\) \[ Q=\begin{bmatrix} q_1 & q_2 & \dotsm & q_{n_2} & \dotsm & q_n \end{bmatrix} \] Let \(q_{n_2+1}\) ~ \(q_n\) be such vectors

other vectors are chosen arbitrarily in order to make Q nonsingular.

Then we have \[ \bar A=Q^{-1}AQ \\ \bar B=Q^{-1}B \\ \bar C=CQ\\ \bar D=D \\ \bar x=Q^{-1}x \\ \dot {\bar x} =Q^{-1}AQ\bar x+Q^{-1}Bu \\ y=CQ\bar x \]

Reachability

A state \(x_l\) is reachable if there exists a u(t) such that \[ x_l=\int_0^le^{A(l-t)}Bu(t)dt \] The set of reachable state, R, is the collection of all \(x_l\) over all possible.

Note that R is a linear subspace of \(R^n\) for all \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). \[ \alpha x_a+\beta x_b \]

The set of reachable state \(R\) , \(R=\text{Range space of } W(0,l)\)

Minimal Realization

A realization (A, B, C, D) of a transfer function/matrix H(s) is said to be minimal if no other realization of H(s) has smaller dimension.

(A, B) is controllable and (C, A) is observable are equivalent to (A, B, C, D) is minimal realization.

A transfer function cannot be realized in minimal realization if it has same poles and zeros.

Kalman Decomposition can reduce a state space to minimal realization.

Example \[ G(s)=\frac{s-3}{s^2+2s-15}\\ \text{Realized in Observable Realization}\\ A= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 15\\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} B= \begin{bmatrix} -3\\1 \end{bmatrix} C= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \text{Check Controllability}\\ M_c= \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 15\\ 1 & -5 \end{bmatrix}\quad\text{Uncontrollable}\\ \text{Not Minimal Realization}\\ P^{-1}= \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1\\ 1 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}P= \begin{bmatrix} -0.25 & 0.25\\ 0.25 & 0.75 \end{bmatrix}\\ \bar A=PAP^{-1}= \begin{bmatrix} -5 & -4\\ 0 & 3\\ \end{bmatrix}\bar B=PB= \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \bar C=CP^{-1}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \text{Then the minimal realization is}\\ \dot x=-5x+u\quad y=x \]
